What I've Been Reading Lately

Return Home

Much like my advisor1, I’ve been trying to get back into reading books after a hiatus implicitly imposed by engineering and computer science curricula2, and then by being busy. A side effect of the double-whammy of quarantine and exploration of my gender expression/identity, however, is that I’ve had to cut pretty much everything that doesn’t give me space/energy/spoons during restful time so I don’t have a meltdown halfway through the week. Oodles and oodles of fun. This hasn’t been all bad – I’m doing more yoga, meditation, tarot readings, basically a smörgåsbord of femme PNW actives (I know, I’ve really come into my own). TV’s become a bit too overstimulating, unfort, as has surfing the internet, so I’ve had to resort more to books and music than I have in a while3.

I’ve been leaning away from Spotify towards Bandcamp 4, and so most of my music interests post-2015 are over there, but Goodreads is just so damn terrible; I just want a little virtual bookshelf that’s not being scraped and productized. And, hey, I’m proud of myself for finally getting into books, which feels like such a weird thing to say, but I’ve had so many on and off points of reading in my life that I’m really enjoying some stability here.

If there’s something that’s not here that you think I’d really enjoy, please please let me know!

What I’m Reading Now

  • Dune Messiah, Frank Herbert
  • Manifestingly Haraway, Donna J. Haraway. The Cyborg Manifesto is absolutely amazing, in the way that somewhat dense, philosophical, deeply metaphorical academic writing can be profoundly empowering – this is a collection of that essay (which I’ve read a few times, but wanted a copy that wasn’t a PDF printout), The Companion Species Manifesto, and an interview transcript; it seemed fun.

What I’ve finished somewhat recently

  • Kaibyo: The Supernatural Cats of Japan, Zack Davidson.
  • Mrs Dalloway, Virginia Woolf
  • Breakfast with Buddha, Roland Merullo. My mom thought that it’d be good for me to have a more casual book, which y’all should take as a huge indication that we, as a family, take ourselves way to seriously. The first 60 or so pages weren’t the most fun to get through, but the book moves into casual philosophizing at various aspects of cross-country road travel. The ending wasn’t great; really, good while the story’s moving (literally), not so good while it’s stagnant.
  • Pedagogy of the Oppressed, Paulo Freire. I’ve been meaning to read this for ages, I biked to Third Place Books in Seward Park to pick up a copy. Totally awesome, situated qualitative work (or therapy) can be the tools of the revolution.
  • The Princess Bride, William Goldman. I was 100% duped about the whole S. Morgenstern thing, but wonderful.
  • Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone, J.K. Rowling. J.K. Rowling’s kind of terrible, but the books were so integral to my childhood that I feel like I have to separate artists from work here. Also, the fandom’s (claimed? reclaimed?) so much here: see the definitive Hogwarts House Quiz, with explanations. I picked up a used copy at Couth Buzzard Books in Greenwood back in August.
